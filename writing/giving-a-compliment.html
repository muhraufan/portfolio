Giving a compliment and how to react to it.

This started as a Twitter thread — a collection of observations about how compliment culture differs between Indonesia and Japan, and how navigating that difference shaped my thinking about professional relationships.

Be Authentic

The most powerful compliments are the genuine ones. When you tell someone their work was impactful, it only lands if you mean it. Empty praise is easy to detect — especially in cross-cultural settings where people are already attuned to subtle signals of insincerity.

Authenticity doesn't mean being excessive. One honest, specific observation will always outperform three generic words of encouragement.

Be Specific

"Good job" is forgettable. "The way you structured that presentation made a complex problem feel instantly clear — that took real craft" is memorable.

Specificity shows you were paying attention. It communicates respect — that the other person's work was worth noticing in detail. In Japan especially, where people often understate their own achievements, a specific compliment carries extra weight because it acknowledges something the person themselves might not have highlighted.

Keep it Simple

Over-elaborating can make a compliment feel performative. Say what you mean, mean what you say, and stop there. The goal is to make someone feel seen — not to turn a compliment into a speech.

Share compliments with people you work with

Don't save appreciation for annual reviews or special occasions. Make it part of your daily practice. The best teams I've worked on had cultures where positive feedback flowed as naturally as constructive feedback.

This is especially true in remote or cross-timezone environments where casual "great work" moments don't happen organically in the hallway. You have to be intentional about creating them.

Don't turn down a compliment

This one surprised me when I first moved to Japan. There's a cultural tendency (in both Indonesian and Japanese contexts, but differently expressed) to deflect compliments — to minimize one's own achievement out of humility.

But deflecting a compliment can actually make the giver feel awkward — as if their observation was wrong. The better response is simple: "Thank you." Receive it graciously. You can share credit ("I couldn't have done it without the team") without dismissing the recognition.

Good design is built on clear communication — and that includes the communication of appreciation. How we give and receive compliments shapes team culture, trust, and ultimately, the quality of work we produce together.
