Considering cultural background in design.

This piece started as a sharing session with eFishery — a reflection on what I've learned designing across Indonesia and Japan, and why culture should be a first-class consideration in any design process.

1. Why It's Important

Design is communication. And communication is deeply shaped by cultural context. What feels intuitive in one culture can feel foreign or even offensive in another. A product that works beautifully for Japanese users might feel sterile and cold to Indonesian users who prefer warmth and personality in their interfaces.

The stakes are real. Walmart entered Japan with the same big-box warehouse model that succeeded in the US — and failed. Japanese consumers value quality presentation and service above bulk discount pricing. The cultural mismatch was fatal. Ikinari Steak, a standing steak restaurant that thrived in Japan, expanded to the US and struggled — the casual standing format didn't match American expectations of a steakhouse experience.

Design makes the same mistakes when it ignores culture.

2. Localization is Not Translation

Translation is changing words. Localization is changing meaning.

Inside Out (Pixar) changed the disgusting food from broccoli to bell pepper for the Japanese market — because Japanese kids like broccoli. A small change with a big impact on how the story landed emotionally.

Compare Yahoo Japan and Google Japan. Yahoo Japan kept a dense, information-rich portal layout long after Yahoo US simplified. Why? Because Japanese users, accustomed to information-dense environments (dense cityscapes, layered kanji characters, packed train timetables), were comfortable — even preferred — higher information density.

Emoji use is another example. The Western 😊 is warmth and positivity. In Japan, ^_^ (made from text characters) carries the same meaning but through a completely different visual grammar — one rooted in a distinct culture of emoticons that predates the emoji standard.

Good localization requires understanding why people behave as they do, not just copying their surface-level preferences.

3. What I Do to Understand

After working across Indonesia, Japan, and the US, here are the three practices that have served me most:

Read more

Books, articles, and research about the culture you're designing for. Not travel guides — anthropology, sociology, and local literature. Understanding how people think requires going beyond what's visible.

Ask more

Local users, local colleagues, local partners. Ask them not just what they prefer, but why. The "why" is where cultural insight lives. A Japanese colleague once explained to me why a certain app felt rude — it was using informal language register in a context that called for formal. I wouldn't have caught that from a usability test alone.

Reflect more

Be aware of your own cultural defaults. As a designer, your instincts were formed by your own cultural experience. What feels "obviously right" to you may be a culturally specific intuition — not a universal truth. Regular reflection on your own assumptions is how you keep them from silently shaping your work.

Culture is the invisible layer of every design decision. The designers who do the most meaningful work are those who take the time to make it visible.
